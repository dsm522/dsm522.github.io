🪦 Your AI pilot isn't failing because of the tech

It's going to die in a drawer because of the people.

After spending 25 years delivering technology projects, I've seen the same pattern repeat itself so many times it's almost predictable:

Leadership gets excited. Lots of fuss is created. Something gets prototyped in a sandbox. And then... it quietly fizzles out.

The graveyard isn't full of bad ideas or broken tech. It's full of projects that ignored a fundamental industry reality: Successful AI adoption is 20% technology and 80% change management and execution.

When we treat AI purely as a tech experiment rather than a rigorous business delivery, we stumble into the exact same traps that kill any major initiative:

**No clear problem statement:** "Explore how AI could help us" is a holding pattern, not a brief. Every successful delivery starts with a specific business problem and a specific end-user. If you are just building a solution looking for a problem, you are building a toy.

**Ignoring the 80% (The Humans):** You can build the most elegant model in the world (the 20%), but if you don't design it with the end-users and adapt their workflows, they will reject it. You cannot code your way to user adoption or trust.

**No accountable owner:** When an initiative belongs to a cross-functional committee, it belongs to no one. Projects don't survive the leap from pilot to production without a single, named human who is personally accountable for the outcome.

**No way to measure progress:** You can't manage what you don't measure. Most AI pilots never define what "working" looks like upfront — so they can never prove ROI, and they can never justify the next phase of investment.

These aren't AI problems. They're *delivery* problems.

The organisations getting real value from AI aren't doing anything magical. They're just applying the same discipline that makes any complex initiative succeed: clear scope, clear ownership, clear metrics, and a relentless focus on the humans using the tool.

Stop treating your AI pilots like science fair projects, and start treating them like real deliveries.
