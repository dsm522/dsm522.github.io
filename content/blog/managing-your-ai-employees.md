You wouldn't hire someone, hand them a stack of work, and then never check the output.

So why are people doing exactly that with AI?

---

## The unmanaged AI problem

Imagine your AI employee writes 50 customer emails a day. How many are you reading?

If the answer is zero, you've got a staff member with no manager. No oversight. No quality control. Just output, going out the door, unchecked.

This isn't a hypothetical risk. It's already happening in organisations that have rushed to deploy AI tools without thinking about governance. And when it goes wrong — hallucinated information, off-brand tone, factually incorrect responses — the damage is real.

---

## Observability isn't a buzzword. It's management basics.

In software engineering, **observability** is the practice of understanding what a system is doing from the outside — through logs, metrics, and monitoring. You build it in from the start, not as an afterthought.

The same principle applies to AI employees. You need to know:

- **Is the work being done correctly?**
- **How do you catch mistakes before they reach customers?**
- **How do you improve performance over time?**

With human employees, we have reviews, spot checks, peer feedback, and the kind of intuition that comes from working alongside someone. You notice when something feels off.

With AI employees, you need **the same discipline — built into the system.**

---

## What this looks like in practice

**Spot checks.** Review a sample of AI outputs regularly — not just when something breaks. Build this into your workflow. Set a cadence. Make it someone's job.

**Clear success criteria.** Before you deploy an AI agent, define what "good" looks like. If you can't articulate success criteria, you can't evaluate anyone — human or AI. This goes back to the engineering mindset: define outputs before you build the process.

**Feedback loops.** When an AI employee gets it wrong, does the correction feed back into how it works next time? If you're just fixing individual mistakes without improving the underlying system, you're managing symptoms, not causes.

**Escalation paths.** What happens when the AI hits an edge case it can't handle? Is there a clear handoff to a human? Or does it just fail silently?

---

## This is delivery management

I've been doing this for 20 years. The AI part is new. The discipline isn't.

The companies racing to deploy AI without observability are the ones who'll be debugging hallucinated reports at 2am, wondering what went wrong. The ones building evaluation into their workflows from day one? They're building something that actually scales.

There's also a broader point here about **trust**. The way you build justified trust in an AI system is the same way you build trust in a new hire — gradual expansion of autonomy, based on demonstrated performance, with ongoing oversight. You don't hand a new employee the keys on day one. You shouldn't do it with AI either.

---

## The emerging discipline

What I'm describing doesn't have a settled name yet. Some people call it AI Ops. Others call it AI delivery management, or AI governance. The terminology will standardise over time.

But the core competency — knowing how to structure work, define quality, build oversight, and create feedback loops — is something that delivery managers, programme managers, and operations leads already possess.

The question isn't whether your organisation needs this discipline. It's whether you're building it deliberately, or waiting until something goes wrong.

Managing AI employees isn't that different from managing human ones.

You just can't rely on them telling you when they're struggling.
