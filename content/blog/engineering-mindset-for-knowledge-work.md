The biggest barrier to AI adoption isn't the technology.

It's that most organisations **can't describe what they actually do.**

---

## What software engineers figured out decades ago

Think about how software engineering matured. We learned — often the hard way — that if you want reliable, repeatable results, you need:

- **Clear inputs and outputs** — what goes in, what comes out
- **Defined processes** — not "Steve handles that" but documented steps anyone can follow
- **Documentation** that a new hire can pick up on day one
- **Error handling** — what happens when things go wrong

We didn't develop these practices because engineers are pedantic. We developed them because **code doesn't read your mind.**

Here's the thing: **AI employees don't read your mind either.**

---

## The gap where AI adoption fails

I've spent 20 years on both sides of this — writing the code, then managing the work that code delivers. The gap between those two worlds is exactly where most AI adoption fails.

The organisations that will get the most from AI aren't the ones buying the fanciest tools. They're the ones applying **engineering discipline to knowledge work** — breaking tasks into clear steps, defining what "done" actually looks like, and documenting the context that currently lives only in people's heads.

Here's a test: if a process only works because Sarah "just knows" how to do it, no AI is going to replace Sarah. 

But if you can write it down clearly enough for a new hire to follow on day one? An AI employee can run it today.

---

## Who's actually positioned to make this work?

Here's the counterintuitive truth: **the people best placed to make AI work aren't the engineers building it.**

They're the people who already know how to structure work.

Programme managers. Delivery leads. Operations people. Business analysts. The people who have spent years turning vague business intent into something that can actually be executed — by humans, by systems, or now, by AI agents.

The AI part is new. The discipline isn't.

---

## What this means practically

If you're trying to adopt AI in your organisation, start here:

1. **Pick a process** that's repetitive, high-volume, and reasonably well understood
2. **Write it down** — every step, every decision point, every exception
3. **Define what good looks like** — not "Sarah will know," but measurable success criteria
4. **Test it with a human first** — if a new hire couldn't follow it, an AI won't either
5. **Then** hand it to an AI

This isn't glamorous. It's not the part that gets written up in the AI hype cycle. But it's the work that actually determines whether AI adoption delivers value — or just adds a new layer of confusion on top of existing chaos.

The engineering mindset isn't just for engineers anymore. It's the foundation for delegating to AI.
