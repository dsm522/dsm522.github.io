Way back when we were building C++ GUI apps on ISDN terminals, this thing called the internet was becoming interesting. Niche, but interesting.

Fresh out of university, I'd been fed the latest in object-oriented thinking — but secretly spent most evenings in the dark underworld of FTP, Usenet and IRC.

My first job was a safe bet. Building those GUI apps, steady work, solid company. But I soon felt stuck — like rush hour on the Tokyo subway. I knew I had to dive into this internet thing. So I moved to a startup purely on the promise of "I'm a fast learner." I grinded. I grew the company. Built some genuinely cool products — payment systems, music streaming, video platforms — before most people had broadband. Then everything became internet-ised, and that bet paid off.

For the next couple of decades, things gradually evolved. Plenty of incremental value, but nothing that fundamentally changed how work was done. I was also raising a family, which is a small distraction, to put it mildly.

---

## Here we go again

You know where this is going.

Yes, I'm talking about AI — but specifically **AI with agency**. Agentic AI.

Having followed technology for 40 years, this excites me enough to pivot again. And, honestly, scares the living daylights out of me.

I'm doing an AI & Business Transformation course at Imperial Business School. It's valuable, but it won't be enough. This paradigm shift turns how we work — and how work gets done — completely on its head.

I've been running an AI agent (my "first AI employee") for a few weeks now. Here's what I've learned:

- **Current LLMs are more than capable** of complex tasks, not just coding
- **AI agents are excellent 24/7 employees** — tireless, consistent, always on
- **Software can now be truly personal** — I had a custom project tracker built in about 10 minutes
- **You don't need specialist skills** to do this — structured thinking matters more than technical depth

---

## The uncomfortable truth

This change is happening faster than 90%+ of organisations will adapt. It's more akin to an ongoing rug-pull than a gradual transition. 

I've run change management and transformation programmes. Forget the traditional playbook — you're either doing this already, or you're already behind. This is not a fad. It's not a bubble. It's happening.

**In the near term**, particularly in software and digital services:

- Larger enterprises will be too slow to react, and there will be casualties
- Savvy startups will thrive — a new gold rush has started
- More "employees" will be AI agents
- New job titles will come and go (and most of the trendy ones won't stick)

**Longer term:**

- Work culture, hierarchies, and many job roles will fundamentally change — it becomes humans and agents, working together
- The cost of software will approach near-zero for many use cases

---

## So what's the answer?

If you're looking for a tidy solution at the end of this post, here it is:

**Get your trainers on.**

This isn't a spectator sport. The people who will thrive are the ones who are already doing it — experimenting, failing, learning, iterating. Not the ones waiting for their organisation to issue a policy.

I've made this pivot before. I'm making it again. The skills that got me here — pattern recognition, delivery rigour, knowing how to make technology work inside real organisations — are exactly what's needed to navigate what comes next.

The only question is whether you're moving fast enough.
